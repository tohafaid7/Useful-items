speech deficits associated with bilateral thalamotomy in Parkinson’s disease: effects of intensive voice therapy.(case study during 3years)

Abstract:
This structured abstract reviews the effects of intensive voice therapy on speech deficits in Parkinson's disease (PD) patients following bilateral thalamotomy. The study focuses on one case study and explores the use of the Lee Silverman Voice Treatment (LSVT) in improving speech performance and vocal intensity, as well as the Voice Handicap Index-10 scores. The paper acknowledges the limitations of current research and emphasizes the need for a comprehensive understanding of prognostic variables that may influence therapy outcomes. It also calls for the development of combined treatment approaches and more advanced delivery methods, such as smartphone-based video conferencing. Additionally, the paper highlights the importance of extended research on the effects of intensive voice therapy, specifically LSVT, on swallowing functions in PD treatment protocols. While the results indicate promising improvements in speech problems, further research is needed to fully understand the therapeutic implications of intensive voice therapy for PD patients

The discussion and implications

(Hongli et al., 2018)

of the effects of intensive voice therapy on speech deficits in Parkinson's

disease can be interpreted based on the available data.

The Lee Silverman Voice

Treatment (LSVT) has been identified as an effective behavioral intervention

approach for treating speech and vocal difficulties in patients with

Parkinson's disease (PD).

The treatment focuses on

improving vocal loudness and enhancing other vocal features.

Several studies have shown

positive results from LSVT, including improvements in vocal intensity,

fundamental frequency, and overall intelligibility. These improvements have

been observed both immediately after the intervention and up to one year

post-treatment. The treatment effects are associated with large effect sizes,

indicating a strong degree of practical significance.

(Büssing et al., 2008)(Cheng & Su, 2020)

It is worth noting that speech therapies focusing solely on speed and articulation have presented limited effectiveness in improving the symptoms of PD in the long term. However, LSVT stands out due to its specific features: it targets the voice as the primary intervention target, it is a high-effort and intensive therapy, and it emphasizes calibration to promote generalization and long-term effects.

(Mohseni et al., 2022)

LSVT has been found to be more effective than respiration treatment alone for improving vocal intensity and decreasing the impact of Parkinson's disease on communication. The treatment seems to be particularly effective for increasing speaking intensity during sustained vowel production.

(Fujii & Wan, 2014)

This may be attributed to the focus on rescaling vocal effort during speech, which recruits cognitive pathways aligned with direct activation pyramidal pathways.

(Arriaga et al., 2012)

Considering future treatment options, it is important to address some limitations of research on LSVT programs. Prognostic variables for treatment response need to be better defined, taking into account factors such as depression, dementia, orthopedic

(Jiang et al., 2023)

complications, and atypical PD. Additionally, future research should investigate combined treatment approaches involving pharmacological, surgical, and speech therapies.

(Hongli et al., 2018)

Overall, the results obtained

from case studies and randomized controlled trials support the effectiveness of

intensive voice therapy like LSVT in mitigating speech deficits in patients with PD.

The treatment shows promise i improving vocal loudness and other vocal features, thereby positively impacting

(Fabron et al., 2018)

social interactions and quality of life for these individuals. Further research

and implementation of combined treatment approaches are key considerations for

future advancements in the field.

8. Conclusion

In conclusion, intensive voicetherapy has shown promising effects on speech deficits in Parkinson's disease patients with bilateral thalamotomy.

Studies have demonstrated improvements in vocal loudness, speech intelligibility, and articulatory changes following intensive voice treatment such as the Lee Silverman Voice Treatment (LSVT).

(Prenger et al., 2020)(Butala et al., 2022)

This high-effort and intensive therapy focuses on calibrating the voice and has

(McInerney et al., 2021)

been found to have long-term effects on vocal features.

However, further research and development in this area are crucial. While the LSVT has shown effectiveness in improving vocal loudness and other vocal features, there is still a need to better define prognostic variables for treatment response in individuals with various factors such as depression, dementia, apathy, orthopedic complications, dyskinesias, atypical PD, and post-DBS surgery.

Additionally, more studies are required to assess the effects of intensive voice therapy on swallowing function in Parkinson's disease patients.

Speech therapy approaches that target speed and articulation have presented limited effectiveness and unsuccessful long-term effects. Therefore, a behavioral method like the LSVT focuses on intensity of laryngeal function and emphasizes calibration appears

(Dias et al., 2016)

to be a more effective approach. The LSVT is an intensive intervention that has

been shown to improve loudness and enhance other vocal features up to several

months after treatment.

In summary, intensive voice

therapy shows promise in addressing speech deficits in Parkinson's disease

(Franzoi et al., 2018)

patients with bilateral thalamotomy. However, further research is needed to

better understand treatment outcomes in individuals with different characteristics

and to explore the effects of intensive voice therapy on swallowing function.

(Franzoi et al., 2018)

By continuing to study and develop evidence-based treatment programs like the

LSVT, we can improve the quality of life for individuals with Parkinson's

disease.
